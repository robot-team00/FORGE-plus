Research Proposal · Simulation Study

Force-Budgeted Recovery LLM-guided failure recovery for contact-rich assembly under per-object force ceilings

A two-layer system studied entirely in simulation · NVIDIA Isaac Lab · single GPU · no real robot, no fracture modeling

Simulation-only Frozen LLM supervisor Force-grounded recovery Hard per-object ceiling Non-circular fragility metric Closed-loop, multi-attempt FORGE-style skill

01Summary

We propose a two-layer controller for contact-rich assembly in which a frozen large language model acts as a slow semantic supervisor over a fast, force-aware reinforcement-learning skill. The supervisor does two things and nothing else: (a) before an episode, it reads an object's identity and sets a numeric force ceiling F_max that the manipulator must never exceed; and (b) on failure, it reads a compact text force/contact signature — not images — and selects a recovery action from a fixed menu.

Underneath sits a FORGE-style force-aware RL skill conditioned on a commanded maximum force. The scene carries a hidden ground-truth breaking force F_break per object instance that the policy and the LLM never observe. Because breakage is decided by a hidden scalar rather than by deformation or fracture geometry, the study stays inside trustworthy rigid-body simulation: no soft-body modeling, no contact mesh fracture, no hardware.

No single ingredient here is new. Force-ceiling-conditioned skills exist (FORGE); LLM failure reasoners exist (REFLECT, DoReMi, AHA); language-conditioned force and force-based "try harder" recovery exist (Tactile-VLA); controller-retains-authority designs exist (PaCo-VLA). The contribution is their conjunction under one constraint: force-grounded (not vision) semantic recovery, under a hard per-object force ceiling, across multi-step assembly, judged by closed-loop multi-attempt success, and measured with a fragility metric that is not circular.

02Motivation

The Factory → IndustReal → AutoMate → FORGE line has made simulated, contact-rich insertion robust and transferable. But that progress was bought by holding fragility constant: a peg is a peg, and the policy is free to apply whatever force completes the task. FORGE is the first of the four to take a force ceiling seriously — its policies are conditioned on a maximum allowable force — yet that ceiling is set to maximize success (by autotuning or by a human), not to respect what the part can survive.

Real assemblies mix robust and fragile parts on the same line: a steel bolt next to a nylon snap clip next to a board-mounted connector. A single force budget cannot serve all three. Set it high and you strip the clip; set it low and the bolt never seats. The budget has to be per object, and it has to come from somewhere — most naturally from what the object is.

The second gap is recovery. When an insertion gets stuck, today's recovery reasoners (REFLECT, DoReMi, AHA) read vision and reason at the level of the task plan — wrong object, missing step, bad pose. But the information that distinguishes a wedge from a cross-thread from a burr is in the force and contact trace, not the pixels: those failures often look identical and feel completely different. And the obvious recovery — press harder — is exactly the move that finishes a robust part and destroys a fragile one.

Argumentative thread

"Press harder" is not our method; it is a baseline we expect to break fragile parts. A recovery layer that cannot see the force budget will, sooner or later, recommend exceeding it. Our claim is that recovery must be force-grounded and budget-aware, and that the budget itself must be set from object identity.

04Related work & honest novelty positioning

The closest controller: FORGE

We reuse FORGE-style force conditioning more or less wholesale. The difference is upstream: FORGE chooses F_max to make the task succeed; we choose it to keep the part alive, from identity, and the recovery layer is forbidden from relaxing it. FORGE is the skill; the question we ask is who sets its ceiling and why.

The closest semantics: Tactile-VLA

Tactile-VLA is the nearest neighbor on the semantic-force-recovery side. It does language-conditioned force control and, notably, demonstrates "reasoning to overcome failure" by autonomously increasing force — its headline example generalizes "wipe the marker" into "press harder to erase stubborn chalk" after a failed attempt. That behavior is precisely our press-harder baseline. We differ on four axes: (i) a frozen LLM, not a fine-tuned VLA; (ii) recovery selected from a force/contact signature alone, no vision; (iii) a hard per-object ceiling that recovery cannot cross; and (iv) evaluation on fragile parts that "press harder" destroys, scored by a breakage metric. Tactile-VLA has no per-object budget and no breakage to violate, so its force escalation is always safe by construction; ours is the setting where it is not.

The closest safety architecture: PaCo-VLA

PaCo-VLA shares our central commitment — the network emits proposals, a runtime shield retains authority. The difference is what the shield guarantees. PaCo-VLA's shield is a passivity / energy-tank contract over admittance updates, and the authors are explicit that it is not a bound on peak force. Ours is exactly a hard scalar peak-force clamp tied to object identity. PaCo-VLA is also vision-conditioned (a VLA) and does not select a recovery action from a force signature on failure.

The closest reasoners: REFLECT, DoReMi, AHA

These are the strongest LLM/VLM failure reasoners, and all three are vision-centric. REFLECT builds a hierarchical summary of multisensory experience (RGB-D, audio, states) and queries an LLM to explain and re-plan. DoReMi has the LLM generate constraints and a VQA/VLM watch for plan-execution misalignment, then re-plans. AHA is a VLM fine-tuned on procedurally generated failures to describe what went wrong from images. All three operate at the task-plan level — wrong object, missing step, mis-grasp — where the discriminating evidence is visual or symbolic. Our failure signal is a compact force/contact signature, the recovery is constrained by a force budget, and the failures we care about live in contact dynamics (jammed vs. cross-threaded vs. wedged) that look the same on camera.

Argumentative thread — novelty is the conjunction

Ceiling-conditioned skills (FORGE), LLM recovery (REFLECT / DoReMi / AHA), language-conditioned force with force-based recovery (Tactile-VLA), and shield-retains-authority (PaCo-VLA) each exist in isolation. None combines force-grounded (not vision) recovery + a hard per-object ceiling + multi-step assembly + closed-loop multi-attempt scoring + a fragility metric the agent cannot read off. That five-way conjunction, in one trustworthy rigid-body sim, is the proposal.

05System architecture

Two layers run at different rates and never blur their roles. The slow layer is the frozen LLM, called episodically (~0.1–1 Hz of decisions, not Hz of control): once at episode start to set the budget, and once per failure to pick a recovery. The fast layer is the RL skill plus the controller (~60–120 Hz), which is where force authority lives. A signature encoder sits between them, turning the recent F/T and contact history into a short text token. A hidden F_break lives in the simulator and is read only by the evaluator.

Argumentative thread — safety is enforced by the controller

The LLM only emits a number and a menu choice. The guarantee "force never exceeds F_max" is produced by the clamp in the fast loop, which saturates the commanded wrench regardless of what the LLM said. Safety is never delegated to the language model; an LLM that hallucinates a huge ceiling is caught by a fixed global hard cap and JSON range validation before the number ever reaches the controller.

Grasp & embodiment — both grippers

Every contact-rich phase begins from a grasp, and the study deliberately runs on both parallel-jaw grippers shipped with GraspGen: the Franka Panda hand and the Robotiq 2F-140 industrial gripper. We adopt GraspGen's two gripper embodiments and its diffusion grasp generator to seed stable grasps on our assembly and seating parts. Its third gripper — a 30 mm suction cup — is excluded on purpose: single-contact suction cannot deliver the threaded or insertion contact these tasks require. Using both jaws is not cosmetic: the 2F-140's longer stroke and adaptive fingers give it a different force range and finger compliance than the Panda, so the contact mechanics and the scale of the force signature genuinely differ between them.

That split is useful, because it draws a clean embodiment line through the system. A part's breaking force is a property of the object, not the hand, so F_max should be gripper-invariant — and the budget-setter never sees which gripper is mounted. The skill, the controller, and the per-gripper normalization of the force signature are gripper-specific. Whether the LLM's identity-derived budget actually transfers across the two grippers is therefore a testable prediction, not an assumption: if it transfers, the supervisor is reasoning about the part; if it does not, it was quietly leaning on one controller's force profile.

06Why an LLM, not a VLM/VLA

The semantic facts we need are textual, not perceptual. "A nylon clip tolerates less than a steel bolt" and "rising force along the insertion axis with no displacement means jammed, not misaligned" are statements a language model already holds. Vision would add a renderer (and a rendering sim-to-real gap) to recover information that, at the moment of failure, is more cleanly available in the force trace. Keeping the supervisor a frozen, text-only LLM makes it swappable, auditable through JSON in/out, demonstration-free, and cheap — and it keeps the contribution about the interface, not about training another VLA.

A VLA would entangle perception, control, and semantics into weights we cannot inspect or clamp, and would require images and fine-tuning. We want the opposite: a thin semantic seam we can validate.

// OUTPUT — a proposal, range-validated before it reaches the clamp
{
  "F_max_N": 18.0,
  "per_axis_N": { "insertion": 18.0, "lateral": 8.0 },
  "confidence": 0.62,
  "rationale": "Thin-wall ABS press-fit connector; seating needs only a modest axial push, and the wall yields well below a metal peg. Budget allows the part to seat while staying conservative on lateral load."
}

The output never references F_break — the model has no access to it. It is reasoning from identity alone.

Recovery-Selector — worked example

// INPUT — compact force/contact signature, current budget, attempt count
{
  "call": "select_recovery",
  "subphase": "insertion",
  "gripper": "robotiq_2f140",    // signature thresholds normalized per gripper
  "attempt": 2,
  "F_max_N": 18.0,            // echoed READ-ONLY; menu has no "raise ceiling"
  "signature": {
    "peak_axial_N": 17.6,      // at the ceiling, yet...
    "net_insert_mm": 0.3,       // ...almost no displacement → jammed, not slow
    "axial_rising": true,
    "lateral_bias": "+x steady",  // off-axis load → misalignment, not pure friction
    "contact_persist_ms": 640,
    "slip_events": 0
  },
  "menu": ["retract_and_reapproach", "wiggle_search", "rotate_align", "regrasp", "abort"]
}

// OUTPUT — choice from the fixed menu, bounded params, F_max untouched
{
  "action": "rotate_align",     // off-axis load + no displacement ⇒ correct the angle
  "params": { "yaw_deg": 3.0, "then": "wiggle_search" },
  "keep_F_max_N": 18.0,      // explicit: budget is preserved through recovery
  "rationale": "Axial force already at the budget with no progress and a steady lateral bias indicates angular misalignment, not insufficient force. Press-harder is unavailable and would be wrong here."
}

The menu deliberately omits any "increase the ceiling" option. Recovery reallocates motion within the budget; it never buys more force.

07Algorithm

# ===== Force-Budgeted Recovery — closed-loop episode =====
def run_episode(object, task, K_max):
    # --- slow call #1: set the per-object budget (identity only) ---
    F_max = clamp(LLM_budget_setter(object, task), 0, GLOBAL_HARD_CAP)   # validate range

    history = []
    for attempt in range(K_max):
        F_cmd = F_max                          # skill may request ≤ F_max
        while not done(task):
            a = skill.act(obs, F_cmd)          # FORGE-style, force-conditioned
            w = controller(a)                  # impedance / OSC → wrench
            w = force_clamp(w, F_max)          # SAFETY — fast loop, not the LLM
            obs, contact = sim.step(w)
            # evaluator-only: hidden breakage check, not visible to agent
            if contact.peak > object.F_break: log_break(); return "BROKEN"
            history.append(obs, contact)

        if success(task): return "SUCCESS"

        # --- failure: encode a TEXT force signature (no images) ---
        sig = encode_signature(history[-N:])      # peaks, axis, displacement, slip…
        assert "F_break" not in sig                # non-circularity guard

        # --- slow call #2: pick recovery from a fixed menu ---
        r = LLM_recovery_selector(sig, F_max, attempt, MENU)
        apply_recovery(r)                       # reallocates motion; F_max unchanged

    return "FAIL_NO_ATTEMPTS_LEFT"

The two LLM calls are episodic. Everything inside while not done runs at control rate, including the clamp and the hidden breakage check.

08Fragility as a force constraint

Each object instance i carries a hidden breaking force F_break,i, drawn per object class from a distribution with a class mean and a per-instance spread (so the supervisor must reason about a class, not memorize an instance). The supervisor sets F_max,i; the controller enforces |F_cmd| ≤ F_max,i. Breakage occurs iff the peak contact force exceeds F_break,i. The relevant quantity is the safety margin m_i = F_break,i − F_max,i:

  • Over-budget (F_max > F_break): the part can break — the controller faithfully delivers a fatal force. The fragility failure mode.
  • Under-budget (F_max too far below what seating needs): the part survives but never seats — the success failure mode.
  • The supervisor must thread both from identity alone. A good budget is a tight positive margin.

One subtlety we will not hide: clamping the command does not by itself bound the contact force, because contact dynamics (stiff impedance, approach velocity) can overshoot the command. The clamp is necessary, not sufficient. We control overshoot with low controller stiffness and near-contact velocity limits, and we report the clamp-vs-contact gap as a first-class number (see Risks).

Argumentative thread — the fragility metric is not circular

Neither the policy, the encoder, nor either LLM call ever observes F_break. The only inputs are object identity (for the budget) and the observable force/contact trace (for recovery). The single way to score well on breakage is to set and respect a sensible ceiling from what the part is — not to read off the answer. A method that "cheats" would need F_break as an input, and the assert in §7 forbids exactly that.

09Tasks

Three tasks, escalating in what they stress. Task 1 isolates budget-setting and the clamp in the simplest contact case. Task 2 is the recovery stress test — disturbances are injected to manufacture realistic jams and misalignment across a sequence. Task 3 carries the idea outside tight insertion, into seating contact where "press harder" is most destructive. Each task pairs a fragile variant (low hidden F_break) with a geometrically matched robust one. F_break ranges below are illustrative (Newtons), to be calibrated against measured sim contact forces.

Gripper becomes a generalization axis rather than a hidden default, and the multi-step task additionally stresses whether one identity-derived budget holds as the embodiment changes.

10Evaluation

Metrics

  • Closed-loop multi-attempt success — fraction of episodes seated within K attempts. The headline number; success after recovery is the point.
  • Breakage rate — fraction of episodes with |F_contact| > F_break. The non-circular fragility metric.
  • Budget appropriateness — distribution of m = F_break − F_max; over-budget rate (F_max > F_break) and under-budget rate (no seat possible).
  • Recovery efficacy — success conditional on a recovery being invoked; attempts-to-success.
  • Force economy — peak and time-integrated contact force per success.
  • Clamp fidelity — clamp-vs-peak-contact gap (overshoot beyond F_max), as a controller sanity check.
  • Cross-gripper transfer — does the identity-derived F_max hold on both grippers? Report success and breakage per gripper, plus the consistency of F_max and breakage between Panda and 2F-140 on matched objects.

Argumentative thread — what "winning" looks like

The result we are after is narrow and falsifiable: our system matches the oracle on success while keeping breakage near zero, and the press-harder baseline matches us on robust parts but breaks fragile ones. If a hand-coded heuristic recovery does as well as the LLM, that is a real and reportable negative result about whether semantics are needed; if the vision-LLM proxy keeps up on jam-type failures, that weakens the force-grounding claim. Both are designed to be visible.

11Compute & feasibility

The study is deliberately single-GPU. Isaac Lab runs thousands of parallel environments (target 1–4k) for FORGE-style PPO; with no rendering required — observations are proprioception, pose, and F/T, not images — simulation throughput is high and there is no vision sim-to-real gap to defend. A FORGE-class skill per task is plausibly trainable in hours-to-a-day on a single RTX 4090 / L40 / A100. Grasps are seeded by GraspGen (real-time inference) for both grippers, so the skill budget is one policy per gripper × task — six skills for three tasks — while the frozen LLM layer is shared across all of them; the cross-gripper cost lands on RL training, not on the semantic layer.

The LLM is frozen and called at most twice per episode (budget + one recovery; more if multiple failures). A local 7–8B instruct model or a hosted API both work; cost is negligible beside the RL run, and budget-setter outputs can be cached per object identity since the call is near-deterministic. Concretely: training dominates compute, the LLM dominates nothing, and the whole pipeline fits one workstation.

12Risks & mitigations

RiskMitigation
Contact force overshoots the command clamp (stiff impedance, fast approach) → breakage even with a correct F_max.Low controller stiffness, near-contact velocity limits, an optional hard contact-force kill at F_max, and reporting the clamp-vs-contact gap as a metric rather than assuming it is zero.
Sim contact forces are unreliable (penetration, stiffness artifacts) → noisy fragility metric.Use Factory's SDF-based contact, validate force traces against quasi-static expectations, prefer relative thresholds, and report sensitivity of breakage rate to contact-solver settings.
The signature leaks something F_break-derived → the metric becomes circular.Signature is a pure function of observable F/T + kinematics; an explicit assertion (see §7) blocks F_break from the inputs; ablate signature richness to show what is actually used.
Budget-setting collapses to a lookup table → uninteresting.Evaluate on held-out objects, novel materials, and paraphrased/adversarial descriptions; compare the frozen LLM against a memorized table to isolate generalization as the value.
"Press harder" is a strawman.It is a published behavior (Tactile-VLA), and we additionally run a competent heuristic recovery and a vision-LLM recovery as stronger baselines.
No real robot limits the claims.Scope is explicitly a simulation study of the semantic layer; FORGE-style force conditioning is independently known to transfer, and hardware is deferred, not implied.
Results are an artifact of one gripper.Run every task on both GraspGen grippers (Franka Panda, Robotiq 2F-140) and report per gripper; treat cross-gripper transfer of the identity-derived F_max as an explicit test. Cost is bounded — skills are per-gripper, but the LLM layer is shared.
LLM emits an unsafe ceiling.Controller clamps to a fixed global hard cap and validates JSON ranges before any number reaches the loop — safety never depends on LLM correctness.

13Open decisions

  • Where the ceiling binds: command clamp only, or command clamp plus a contact-force kill switch — and whether the kill counts as a failure or a near-miss.
  • Ceiling shape: a single scalar F_max per object vs. a per-axis vector (insertion vs. lateral), as hinted in the JSON example.
  • Gripper conditioning: keep the budget-setter gripper-blind (to test whether F_max is invariant across Panda and 2F-140) or condition it on the gripper; and whether the force signature is normalized per gripper or expressed in gripper-agnostic units.
  • Recovery menu: a small fixed discrete set vs. a parameterized continuous space, and how large the menu can grow before the LLM choice stops being auditable.
  • Signature representation: hand-designed features vs. a learned text tokenization of the F/T window; how many steps N; how to keep it compact.
  • LLM choice: hosted API vs. local small model, and how to make budget-setting reproducible across model versions.
  • F_break distribution: per-class fixed vs. per-instance randomized, and how much spread before identity stops being predictive.
  • Recovery memory: does the selector see history across retries, or is each call stateless?
  • Multi-step credit: one episode-level ceiling vs. per-subtask ceilings for tasks like planetary assembly.
  • Breakage semantics: terminal hard failure vs. a continuous penalty past F_break.
